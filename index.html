Merit Aid Notes

  • Kentucky: “Bluegrass Spirit” automatic grid. Tops out at $12,500/yr (3.5 GPA + 30 ACT). Presidential and Singletary (full OOS tuition) are competitive, require separate scholarship-section application, and need ACT 31+/33+ respectively.
  • Tennessee: Out-of-State Volunteer Scholarship uses UT Weighted Core GPA (not unweighted). Grid: 3.8+ core + 28 ACT → $3K; 4.0+ core + 30–33 → $9K; 4.0+ core + 34–36 → $18K.
  • UGA: HOPE/Zell Miller for Georgia residents. Zell threshold: 3.7 HOPE GPA and 26 ACT (Georgia raised it from 25 in 2023). HOPE threshold: 3.0 HOPE GPA.
  • Auburn: Published OOS grid (Fall 2026). Automatic at 3.5 GPA: 29–30 ACT → $7K (Academic Charter), 31–32 → $11K (Heritage), 33–34 → $15K (Presidential), 35–36 → $17K. Deadline Dec 1, scores by Jan 12.
  • Clemson: No automatic grid — holistic review. Realistic OOS award at this profile: $0–$6K/yr, clusters $2–$5K. Don't bank on it. National Scholars is separate full-ride competitive track.
  • Purdue / Ohio State / Virginia Tech / NC State / Texas A&M: No published OOS merit grids. All use holistic review with automatic consideration via the EA application; amounts not predictable. Don't bank on it.
  • Wisconsin: Expensive OOS with minimal merit at these stats. Scholarships are through individual Schools/Colleges via WiSH (Feb 1 deadline); UW explicitly notes fewer scholarships available for non-Wisconsin residents.
  • Cornell: Need-based aid only. No merit scholarships at any stat profile. Aid depends entirely on family financial need assessment; Cornell meets 100% of demonstrated need.

Note: Sticker COA = Tuition & Fees + Room & Board + Books/Supplies, sourced from each school's official 2025-26 Cost of Attendance page. Merit estimates are based on the student's current GPA/ACT profile and will shift if scores change.

Ranking Formula

Each school receives a composite score from 1–5 based on four weighted factors. Admission probability is tracked separately via the Scoir Label column and is not part of this score.

Factor Weight Why This Weight
Program 20% Vet program strength and pre-vet infrastructure (access to a DVM is the whole point of undergrad)
Fit 30% How well the school matches Avery’s visit impressions and cultural preferences — manually set per school
Plan B 30% If the DVM path doesn’t happen, the undergrad degree still needs strong career value
Cost 20% Must still afford 4 years of vet school after undergrad

Formula: Score = (Program × 0.20) + (Fit × 0.30) + (Plan B × 0.30) + (Cost × 0.20)

Merit Aid is shown on the Rankings tab for reference but is not part of the composite score. Realistic merit already flows through the Cost factor via the Financial Analysis tab.

Scoring Rubrics (1–5 Scale)

Program

5Guaranteed or early admission to own CVM (e.g., UGA, Clemson guarantee programs)
4Top-ranked CVM with guaranteed interview or strong pipeline (e.g., NC State, Purdue)
3Has an accredited CVM on campus (e.g., Auburn, Tennessee, Missouri)
2No CVM but strong pre-vet advising or regional pipeline to nearby CVM
1No CVM, limited pre-vet infrastructure

Fit

Subjective rating set manually after visits and research. Captures how well the campus culture, student body, and day-to-day experience match Avery’s preferences.

5Strong fit — would be thrilled to enroll; campus, vibe, and academics all resonate
4Good fit — mostly aligned, a couple of minor reservations
3Neutral — acceptable fit, no strong pull in either direction
2Weak fit — would consider only as a last resort
1Poor fit — effectively ruled out based on visit / cultural mismatch

Plan B

5Excellent — degree leads directly to strong careers outside vet (e.g., Purdue engineering)
4Strong — well-regarded program with good job placement
3Good — solid program, regional reputation
2Fair — limited career options if vet school doesn't work out

Cost (value tier)

This is a value tier, not an affordability gate. Any school on this list is fundable; the rating places it on the value-per-dollar spectrum relative to the rest of the list, so the composite score reflects the premium (or discount) a school carries.

5Exceptional value — under $100K (4-yr realistic). Cheapest path to the DVM goal on this list.
4Strong value — $100K–$190K. Meaningfully below the list's middle range; cost is not a real factor.
3Typical — $190K–$215K. Mid-pack for this list; cost is about where you'd expect for the program quality.
2Premium — $215K–$245K. Above-average cost; program quality, fit, or career outcome should justify the premium.
1Elite sticker — over $245K. Top of the cost range; only justified by reach-level prestige or a specific career advantage (e.g., Cornell).

Strategic Fit Labels

The dashboard groups schools into three labels based on the composite score. Fit acts as a soft gate: a school scoring 4.0+ overall but below 3 on fit caps at Strong Fit rather than Priority Target — cultural mismatch shouldn’t produce a #1 recommendation.

Label Criteria
Priority Target Composite score ≥ 4.00 and Fit ≥ 3 — top-tier strategic pick
Strong Fit Composite 3.30–3.99, or 4.00+ with Fit < 3 — solid addition to the list
Happy Landing Composite < 3.30 — acceptable outcome but not a strategic priority

Note: Strategic Fit describes how well the school serves Avery’s plan, not how likely she is to get in. Admission probability lives in the Scoir column. A Priority Target can still be a Reach in Scoir, and a Happy Landing can still be Very Likely.

Admission Strategy

Choosing the right major on your application

The Animal Science Advantage

At schools with "direct-admit" systems (Purdue, Virginia Tech, NC State, etc.), students are admitted to a specific college or major — and the College of Agriculture typically admits at a higher rate than Engineering, Business, or the university overall. Even at "university-wide" schools, selecting Animal Science signals clear intent and aligns Avery with a less competitive applicant pool. This strategy costs nothing.

Data transparency: Very few schools publish college-level admission rates. Where a rate is published by the university, it is marked with a ✓ below. All other advantage assessments are qualitative, based on admission structure (direct-to-major vs. university-wide) and publicly available overall rates.

SchoolHow Admission WorksOverall RateAg AdvantagePrimary MajorPre-Vet TrackBackup Major

Can Avery Switch Majors Once Enrolled?

From Animal Science TO Biology/Biochemistry: EASY at all schools — these are lateral moves within related colleges.

From Animal Science TO Engineering/CS/Business: DIFFICULT at Purdue, Texas A&M, NC State, Virginia Tech — competitive internal transfer processes.

Bottom Line: Since Avery is committed to pre-vet, this risk is essentially zero. The strategy has no downside for her goals.
